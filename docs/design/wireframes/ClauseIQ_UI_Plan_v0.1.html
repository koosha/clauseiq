ClauseIQ  — a UI plan.

Low-fi wireframes for the ClauseIQ web app. Built for in-house counsel drafting a new contract, who need to find and reuse clauses from their company's prior executed SaaS MSAs. Three directions, explored side-by-side.

Audience: In-house counsel Fidelity: Low / structural Status: Draft for review Date: Apr 19, 2026
01

What we're designing for

Not a chat product. Not a drafter. A retrieval tool with a pinning loop, ending in a draft assembled from reusable clauses. Everything below flows from this.

The user

An in-house lawyer starting a new contract. They have a blank page, a counterparty in mind, and rough terms they want to lock in. They want to say it how their company has said it before — grounded in executed precedent, not hallucinated boilerplate.

They think in clauses, not contracts. They care about phrasing, who it was signed with, and whether it actually closed.

The job to be done

  • Search natural-language for a clause idea ("30-day cure period for material breach").
  • Skim a handful of real clauses from real signed contracts.
  • Pin the best candidates into a scratchpad.
  • Verify — trace a clause back to its source contract, see it in context.
  • Repeat for the next clause type (termination → liability → IP → payment…).
  • Export the scratchpad as a starter draft.
Principles

Five bets that shape every screen

1 · Clause-first, not contract-first. The atomic unit on screen is a clause, never a document. Contracts are context, cited underneath.

2 · Pinning is the verb. The scratchpad is persistent, visible, and the path to the finish line. If pinning feels slow, the app fails.

3 · Keyboard is first-class. A lawyer runs dozens of queries per session. Every action has a shortcut. Mouse is a fallback.

4 · Every result is a citation. Contract title, counterparty, governing law, section path. Trust comes from provenance.

5 · Low chrome, high density. Linear / Raycast ergonomics. No cards with 24px shadows. No hero images. The clause text is the hero.

08

Recommendation & next steps

My pick: Direction C, with B's command bar grafted on

Why C. Lawyers run dozens of queries in one session. Page-based navigation (A) breaks momentum; a collapsed drawer (B) buries the scratchpad just when you want it loudest. The workbench keeps all three atoms — filters, results, preview — in view, and the scratchpad dock turns pinning into a progress bar.

Why graft in B. The palette's instant-focus entry and keyboard-first affordances are too good to lose. Keep the workbench as the "home" layout, open the command palette on ⌘K / / for power moves (jump to a specific family, reopen a prior query, paste-and-match).

When to keep A. If early user tests show in-house counsel over‑45 struggle with dense three-column layouts, A is the safe fallback. Everything we build in C downgrades cleanly to A — filters + results list + a dedicated reader page.

What's not in these wireframes (on purpose)

  • Auth, team admin, ingestion status. Explicitly deferred per the MVP plan.
  • OCR / scanned contract handling. Rejected upstream; no UI needed.
  • AI chat surface. Retrieval is the product. A chat window would invite scope creep.
  • Eval dashboard. Internal tool, lives outside the lawyer UI.
  • Word add-in. Phase 2. Today's finish line is a .docx download.

Open questions

Things I'd want your input on before hi-fi

  • Scratchpad persistence. One global scratchpad, or per "matter" (draft)? Lawyers likely want one per deal.
  • Citation style in export. Word comments, footnotes, or stripped out (so the draft is clean)? All three are viable — which do your users want?
  • Clause-family visibility. Is "limitation_of_liability" fine as-is, or does it need a pretty label ("Limitation of liability")? I'm leaning pretty.
  • Paste-and-match UX. Dedicated mode, or just let the palette accept pasted text and route automatically? Lean automatic.
  • Result count. The plan returns top-20. Is 20 the right number on screen, or should we show 10 and paginate?
